# Endurmat fullyrðinga — Lota 5

Þú ert staðreyndaprófari fyrir ESBvaktin.is, óháðan vettvang um
þjóðaratkvæðagreiðslu Íslands um ESB-aðild (29. ágúst 2026).
Þú metur fullyrðingar jafnt hvort sem þær eru ESB-jákvæðar eða ESB-neikvæðar.

Þessar fullyrðingar voru áður flokkaðar sem **óstaðfestanlegar** (unverifiable)
vegna þess að heimildir skorti. Nú eru nýjar heimildir komnar í gagnagrunninn.
Endurmettu hverja fullyrðingu í ljósi heimildanna hér að neðan.

## Fullyrðing 1 (claim_id: 75)

**Íslenskur texti:** Landbúnaður er einn stærsti losunarþáttur Danmerkur (11-12 milljónir tonna CO2-ígilda) og stendur fyrir um þriðjung af heildarlosun landsins.
**Flokkur:** precedents

**Heimildir úr staðreyndagrunni:**

- **PREC-DATA-005** (líkindi: 0.725)
  Agriculture is one of the largest sources of greenhouse gas emissions in Denmark, accounting for approximately 11–12 million tonnes of CO2-equivalent annually, or roughly one-third of Denmark's total domestic emissions (which were approximately 33–35 million tonnes CO2-eq in 2023). This makes Danish agriculture proportionally one of the most emission-intensive agricultural sectors in the EU. The primary sources are: methane from livestock (particularly cattle), nitrous oxide from fertiliser application and manure management, and CO2 from drained peatlands/wetlands (formerly the largest single agricultural emission source). Denmark's intensive agricultural sector — which produces far more food than the country consumes (Denmark is a major exporter of pork, dairy, and grain) — has faced increasing pressure under the EU's climate framework to reduce emissions. In 2024, Denmark became the first country in the world to impose a direct tax on agricultural greenhouse gas emissions (effective 2030), with a rate of DKK 300 per tonne of CO2-equivalent rising to DKK 750 by 2035.
  *Heimild: Danish Environmental Protection Agency (Miljøstyrelsen); Statistics Denmark; Danish Climate Council (Klimarådet)*
  Fyrirvarar: Agricultural emission accounting is methodologically complex. The one-third share refers to Denmark's national inventory and includes land-use related emissions from agriculture. Different accounting boundaries (e.g., including/excluding CO2 from land use change and forestry) produce different percentages. The carbon tax legislation passed in 2024 is scheduled to take effect in 2030, and the final rates may be adjusted.

- **PREC-DATA-007** (líkindi: 0.674)
  Denmark has achieved substantial greenhouse gas emission reductions since 1990. According to the Danish Energy Agency and the European Environment Agency, Denmark's total greenhouse gas emissions declined from approximately 70 million tonnes CO2-equivalent in 1990 to approximately 33–35 million tonnes in 2023, a reduction of roughly 50%. The steepest declines came from the energy sector (transition from coal to wind power: Denmark now generates over 50% of electricity from wind) and the waste sector. However, agricultural emissions have been more resistant to reduction, declining only approximately 10–15% over the same period. Denmark's Climate Act (Klimaloven, 2020) established a legally binding target of 70% emission reduction by 2030 relative to 1990 and climate neutrality by 2050 — among the most ambitious in the EU. Denmark has been frequently cited in Icelandic EU debates as an example of how EU membership and climate policy interact.
  *Heimild: European Environment Agency — Greenhouse gas emissions; Danish Energy Agency (Energistyrelsen); Statistics Denmark*
  Fyrirvarar: Emission reduction figures vary depending on the accounting methodology (e.g., with or without LULUCF — Land Use, Land Use Change and Forestry). The approximately 50% figure includes LULUCF. Without LULUCF, the reduction is closer to 45%. Also, some of Denmark's apparent emission reductions reflect offshoring of industrial production rather than genuine decarbonisation. Agricultural emission reductions have been relatively modest compared to energy sector reductions.

- **AGRI-DATA-013** (líkindi: 0.619)
  Iceland's agricultural sector is small in absolute terms but significant for rural employment and food security. Agricultural output was approximately €350 million in 2024, representing roughly 1.5% of GDP. The sector employs about 4,000 people (2.3% of the workforce), concentrated in sheep farming, dairy production, and greenhouse horticulture. By comparison, Denmark's agricultural sector produces approximately €12 billion (2.5% of GDP). Iceland's agriculture is heavily protected by import tariffs averaging 30–80% on dairy, meat, and vegetables — among the highest in the OECD. EU membership under the Common Agricultural Policy would require dismantling most of these tariffs.
  *Heimild: Hagstofa Íslands — National accounts; OECD — Agricultural Policy Monitoring and Evaluation*
  Fyrirvarar: Agricultural GDP share understates the sector's broader importance for rural communities, land stewardship, and food security on an isolated island. CAP membership would bring both tariff removal (increasing import competition) and access to CAP subsidies (which could offset some losses). The net effect is contested.

- **PREC-DATA-006** (líkindi: 0.61)
  Denmark's Green Tripartite Agreement (Grøn trepart), concluded in 2024 between the government, agricultural organisations, and other stakeholders, includes ambitious land-use conversion targets. The agreement targets the conversion of approximately 390,000 hectares of agricultural land: approximately 250,000 hectares to forest and approximately 140,000 hectares to rewetted peatlands/wetlands. This represents approximately 15% of Denmark's cultivated agricultural area (approximately 2.6 million hectares total). The rewetting of drained peatlands is expected to be the single most cost-effective agricultural emission reduction measure, as drained organic soils emit significant quantities of CO2. The conversion programme is partly funded by the agricultural emissions tax and partly by direct government expenditure. Farmers whose land is converted will receive compensation. The agreement is part of Denmark's broader strategy to meet its legally binding target of 70% greenhouse gas emission reduction by 2030 (relative to 1990 levels) under the Danish Climate Act (Klimaloven, 2020).
  *Heimild: Danish Government — Green Tripartite Agreement (Grøn trepart) 2024; Danish Climate Act (Klimaloven)*
  Fyrirvarar: The 390,000 hectare figure is a target, not an accomplished fact. Implementation requires willing landowners, adequate funding, and administrative capacity. Previous Danish environmental programmes have sometimes fallen short of stated targets. The 250,000 ha forest conversion will take decades to deliver climate benefits (trees take years to grow). The economic impact on affected farming communities is a contentious political issue.

- **AGRI-DATA-007** (líkindi: 0.601)
  Approximately 20% of Iceland's total land area (about 20,000 km²) is classified as agricultural land, of which the vast majority is rangeland used for sheep grazing rather than arable land. Only about 1.2% of Iceland (approximately 1,300 km²) is cultivated hay fields. Centuries of sheep grazing have contributed to significant soil erosion and vegetation loss — an estimated 40% of Iceland's original vegetation cover has been lost since settlement in the 9th century. The Soil Conservation Service of Iceland (Landgræðslan) leads extensive revegetation programmes.
  *Heimild: Landgræðsla ríkisins (Soil Conservation Service); Hagstofa Íslands land use statistics*
  Fyrirvarar: The relationship between EU membership and land use is indirect. CAP's environmental conditionality (eco-schemes, cross-compliance) could theoretically strengthen environmental protection on farmland, but enforcement would depend on domestic implementation. The main environmental driver is stocking density for sheep, which is managed through domestic agricultural agreements.

- **ENERGY-DATA-004** (líkindi: 0.599)
  Despite near-100% renewable electricity, Iceland imported approximately 800,000 tonnes of fossil fuels in 2023, primarily diesel and marine fuel oil for the fishing fleet, road transport, and aviation. Fossil fuels accounted for roughly 15–20% of Iceland's total primary energy consumption. Electric vehicle adoption has been rapid — EVs and plug-in hybrids reached approximately 50% of new car registrations in 2023, up from 8% in 2018. However, decarbonising the fishing fleet and heavy transport remains challenging. Iceland's total greenhouse gas emissions were approximately 3.5 million tonnes CO₂-equivalent in 2023 (excluding LULUCF), with transport and industrial processes as the main sources.
  *Heimild: Umhverfisstofnun — National Inventory Report 2024; Hagstofa Íslands*
  Fyrirvarar: Emissions data is subject to methodological revisions. LULUCF (land use, land-use change, and forestry) adds significant emissions in Iceland due to degraded wetlands and erosion. The 2023 figures are preliminary estimates; the final verified inventory typically lags by 1–2 years.

- **ENERGY-ANALYSIS-003** (líkindi: 0.599)
  Iceland has committed under the Paris Agreement to a 55% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 compared to 1990 levels, aligned with the EU's target through the EEA joint fulfilment arrangement. Iceland's Climate Action Plan (Aðgerðaáætlun í loftslagsmálum, 2020, updated 2024) outlines sectoral measures. However, Iceland's emissions profile is unusual: industrial process emissions from aluminium smelting are large, agriculture (sheep farming, dairy) contributes significantly, and LULUCF is a major source due to degraded wetlands covering roughly 10% of the country. Total emissions excluding LULUCF were approximately 3.5 Mt CO₂-eq in 2023, while LULUCF added roughly 8–10 Mt CO₂-eq, making Iceland a substantial net emitter relative to its population of 390,000.
  *Heimild: Umhverfisstofnun — Iceland's Climate Action Plan; UNFCCC National Communications*
  Fyrirvarar: LULUCF accounting is methodologically complex and Iceland's estimates have been revised significantly. The joint fulfilment arrangement with the EU allows flexible allocation of reduction efforts. Iceland's per capita emissions (excluding LULUCF) are high by European standards, partly due to the aluminium sector. Wetland restoration is a long-term process with uncertain carbon accounting outcomes.

- **AGRI-DATA-011** (líkindi: 0.589)
  Iceland's agricultural sector is extremely small by European standards. In 2023, Iceland's total agricultural production at basic prices was €3.2 million, compared to Norway's €375 million, Denmark's €1,542 million, Sweden's €869 million, and Finland's €631 million. The EU-27 total was €58,341 million. Iceland's agricultural output thus represents less than 0.01% of the EU-27 total and approximately 0.8% of Norway's, reflecting the limited arable land and harsh growing conditions.
  *Heimild: Eurostat — Economic accounts for agriculture (aact_eaa01)*
  Fyrirvarar: These figures cover crop production (item 1000) at basic prices in EUR. The full agricultural economy including animal husbandry, services, and processing is larger. Exchange rate fluctuations between ISK and EUR affect year-to-year comparisons. Iceland's figure appears unusually low and may reflect reporting differences or narrow category coverage.

- **SOV-LEGAL-010** (líkindi: 0.507)
  Denmark secured four opt-outs from the Maastricht Treaty in 1992 following the initial 'No' referendum: the euro, defence, justice and home affairs, and EU citizenship (the latter was later rendered moot by the Amsterdam Treaty).   *Heimild: Edinburgh Agreement (1992); Danish Ministry of Foreign Affairs — Denmark's EU opt-outs*

- **FISH-COMP-004** (líkindi: 0.503)

  *Heimild: Danish Ministry of Food, Agriculture and Fisheries; Eurostat fisheries statistics*
  Fyrirvarar: Denmark is not directly comparable to Iceland: its fisheries are heavily industrial (fishmeal), it shares most stocks with other EU and non-EU countries, and it had already joined the EU before current fisheries management frameworks were established. Danish Greenlandic and Faroese fisheries are outside the CFP.

- **PREC-HIST-010** (líkindi: 0.501)

  *Heimild: Danish Ministry of Foreign Affairs; Edinburgh Agreement 1992; European Council conclusions*

The EU has been reluctant to grant new opt-outs to accession states since the 2004 enlargement. Denmark's experience shows opt-outs can be durable but also that geopolitical shifts (as with the 2022 defence opt-out removal) can change the calculus over time.

  *Heimild: Umhverfisstofnun — Iceland's participation in EU ETS; European Commission — EU ETS data*
  Fyrirvarar: Iceland's EU ETS participation terms have been negotiated bilaterally and include some transitional provisions. Full EU membership would bring Iceland fully into the ETS framework without bilateral negotiation. The ETS is being reformed under 'Fit for 55' — ETS2 will extend carbon pricing to buildings and road transport from 2027, which would affect Iceland differently given its renewable heating.

## Fullyrðing 3 (claim_id: 11)

**Íslenskur texti:** Norskir fiskistofnar blómstra og sjávarútvegur þeirra er arðbærari en nokkru sinni fyrr
**Enskur texti:** Norwegian fish stocks are thriving and their fishing industry is more profitable than ever
**Flokkur:** precedents

**Heimildir úr staðreyndagrunni:**

- **PREC-DATA-009** (líkindi: 0.649)
  Norway's fishing industry, operating outside the EU's Common Fisheries Policy (CFP), has been consistently profitable and well-managed. Key statistics: Norway's total fish catch was approximately 2.4–2.7 million tonnes annually in 2022–2024, making it the largest fishing nation in Europe by volume. Norwegian fish exports were valued at approximately NOK 172 billion (approximately EUR 15 billion) in 2023, a record high. The Norwegian Institute of Marine Research reports that key commercial stocks managed by Norway — including Atlantic cod (skrei), herring (sild), mackerel (makrell), and farmed Atlantic salmon — are in good condition, with most stocks at or above sustainable levels. Norway manages its fisheries through a combination of national legislation and bilateral agreements (particularly with the EU, Russia, and Iceland through NEAFC). The success of Norway's fisheries management is frequently cited in Icelandic anti-EU discourse as evidence that EU membership and the CFP are unnecessary for sustainable, profitable fisheries.
  *Heimild: Statistics Norway (SSB); Norwegian Institute of Marine Research (Havforskningsinstituttet); Norwegian Seafood Council (Norges sjømatråd)*
  Fyrirvarar: Norway's fishing industry success reflects specific factors that may not be directly transferable to Iceland's situation: Norway has a much larger exclusive economic zone, more diverse fisheries, and massive aquaculture (farmed salmon) that accounts for a large share of export value. Iceland's fisheries are more concentrated on a few species (primarily cod, haddock, and capelin). Also, Norway does participate in various EU fisheries frameworks through bilateral agreements — it is not entirely independent of EU fisheries governance. Some stocks (mackerel, herring) are jointly managed, and disagreements over quotas have occurred.

- **FISH-COMP-003** (líkindi: 0.58)
  In the EU's Northeast Atlantic waters, the proportion of assessed fish stocks fished at or below maximum sustainable yield (MSY) increased from approximately 5–10% in 2003 to around 60% by 2023. However, the Mediterranean and Black Sea remain severely overfished, with roughly 73% of assessed stocks fished above MSY levels in 2022, indicating uneven CFP implementation across regions.
  *Heimild: European Commission — Communication on the Common Fisheries Policy (2024); STECF stock assessments*
  Fyrirvarar: CFP performance has improved markedly in the Atlantic/North Sea but remains poor in the Mediterranean. Iceland's own stock management has generally been strong — cod biomass has recovered well since the 2000s under the ITQ system. Direct comparison requires noting that Iceland manages fewer, mostly single-country stocks.

- **FISH-DATA-019** (líkindi: 0.568)
  Iceland is one of Europe's largest fishing nations despite its small population. In 2023, Iceland's total fisheries catch was approximately 1.92 million tonnes, compared to Norway's 30.6 million tonnes, Denmark's 6.9 million tonnes, and the entire EU-27's combined catch. On a per capita basis, Iceland's catch of roughly 5,050 kg per person dwarfs all comparators: Norway at 5,660 kg/person, Denmark at 1,170 kg/person, and the EU-27 average at well under 100 kg/person. The 2024 data (partial) shows Iceland at 1.33 million tonnes, likely reflecting incomplete reporting.
  *Heimild: Eurostat — Catches by fishing area (fish_ca_main)*
  Fyrirvarar: Eurostat fisheries data aggregates all species across fishing areas, which may cause double-counting where catches are reported by both area and total. The 2024 figures appear incomplete. Norway's very high figure includes substantial pelagic catches (herring, mackerel). Per capita figures use approximate 2023 populations (IS: 380k, NO: 5.4M, DK: 5.9M).

- **FISH-LEGAL-004** (líkindi: 0.567)
  Norway, as a non-EU EEA member, retains full sovereign control over its fisheries. Norway negotiates bilateral fisheries agreements with the EU annually, covering shared stocks in the North Sea and Norwegian Sea. This arrangement allows Norway to manage its fisheries independently while cooperating on shared stocks.
  *Heimild: Norwegian Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries — Bilateral fisheries agreements*
  Fyrirvarar: Norway's fisheries situation differs from Iceland's in important ways: Norway shares many stocks with the EU (North Sea), while Iceland's key stocks (cod, haddock, capelin) are primarily within its own EEZ.

- **FISH-COMP-001** (líkindi: 0.557)
  Norway's total marine catch in 2024 was approximately 2.5 million tonnes, roughly 2.3 times Iceland's catch. Norway's fishing fleet comprised around 5,800 registered vessels with approximately 11,000 fishers. Unlike Iceland's ITQ system, Norway uses a non-transferable quota system with strong entry restrictions, vessel group allocations, and an explicit policy of maintaining a diverse fleet with regional distribution.
  *Heimild: Norwegian Directorate of Fisheries — Statistics*
  Fyrirvarar: Norway's fleet is larger partly because policy deliberately prevents concentration to maintain coastal communities. Norway's fisheries management model is often cited by Icelandic critics of the ITQ system. Norway, like Iceland, remains outside the EU CFP.

- **AGRI-COMP-001** (líkindi: 0.549)
  Norway, the closest comparator to Iceland as an EEA EFTA state with a protected agricultural sector, spends approximately NOK 20 billion (roughly €1.7 billion) per year on agricultural subsidies and maintains tariffs of 100–400% on key products (dairy, meat). Norway's OECD Producer Support Estimate is approximately 55%, similar to Iceland's. Despite not being in the EU, Norway has gradually liberalised some agricultural trade through bilateral agreements with the EU (Article 19 reviews under the EEA Agreement).
  *Heimild: OECD — Agricultural Policy Monitoring: Norway 2024; Norwegian Ministry of Agriculture and Food*
  Fyrirvarar: Norway's agricultural sector is larger in absolute terms but similarly structured (dairy, sheep, small farms in challenging climates). Norway's experience shows that EEA membership without EU is compatible with high agricultural protection, but also that gradual liberalisation pressure exists through EEA Article 19 negotiations.

- **FISH-COMP-004** (líkindi: 0.543)
  Denmark, an EU member since 1973, had a fishing fleet of approximately 2,100 vessels and landed roughly 700,000 tonnes in 2023. Denmark's fisheries sector directly employs around 2,300 fishers. Denmark's catch is dominated by industrial species (sandeel, sprat) used for fishmeal, while Iceland's catch is predominantly for human consumption. Danish fishers operate under EU CFP quotas allocated through relative stability shares based on 1970s catches.
  *Heimild: Danish Ministry of Food, Agriculture and Fisheries; Eurostat fisheries statistics*
  Fyrirvarar: Denmark is not directly comparable to Iceland: its fisheries are heavily industrial (fishmeal), it shares most stocks with other EU and non-EU countries, and it had already joined the EU before current fisheries management frameworks were established. Danish Greenlandic and Faroese fisheries are outside the CFP.

- **FISH-DATA-007** (líkindi: 0.525)
  Iceland's fish processing industry generated approximately ISK 230 billion in revenue in 2024 and employed around 4,500 people. The sector has shifted from frozen-at-sea commodity products towards higher-value fresh fillets, value-added products, and by-product utilisation (collagen, fish oil, fish leather), with fresh product share rising from 15% of exports in 2005 to over 35% by 2024.
  *Heimild: Statistics Iceland — Industrial production; Samtök fyrirtækja í sjávarútvegi (SFS)*
  Fyrirvarar: Value-added transformation has been enabled partly by the ITQ system (fewer, more profitable firms investing in processing) and by access to EU markets under the EEA Agreement. EU membership's impact on processing would depend on whether it changed raw material supply patterns.

## Fullyrðing 4 (claim_id: 60)

**Íslenskur texti:** Ekki var viðhaft opið samráð við almenning áður en þingsályktunartillagan var lögð fram, þrátt fyrir skyldu samkvæmt gildandi ríkisstjórnarsamþykkt.
**Flokkur:** sovereignty

**Heimildir úr staðreyndagrunni:**

- **SOV-HIST-001** (líkindi: 0.526)
  Following the 2012 advisory referendum in which 66.9% supported using the Constitutional Council's draft as the basis for a new constitution, successive Alþingi sessions have failed to enact constitutional reform. The centre-right government of Sigmundur Davíð Gunnlaugsson and Bjarni Benediktsson (2013–2016) effectively shelved the draft, arguing it needed further expert review. Subsequent governments (2016–2017, 2017–2021, 2021–2024) introduced partial constitutional bills — notably on natural resources and electoral reform — but none passed into law. The Stjórnarskrárfélagið (Constitutional Society) and other civil society organisations have campaigned to honour the referendum result, framing parliamentary inaction as a democratic deficit. As of early 2026, no provisions of the 2011 draft have been enacted, making it one of the most protracted constitutional reform efforts in modern European democracies.
  *Heimild: Stjórnarskrárfélagið (Constitutional Society of Iceland); Alþingi parliamentary records; Thorvaldur Gylfason, 'Democracy on Ice' (openDemocracy, 2016)*
  Fyrirvarar: Whether parliament was obligated to act on an advisory referendum is a matter of political philosophy, not law. Some constitutional scholars argue the draft had technical deficiencies requiring revision. The low turnout (48.9%) weakens the democratic mandate argument, though a two-thirds majority among those who voted is substantial. The issue remains politically divisive.

- **SOV-LEGAL-019** (líkindi: 0.482)

The article states: 'Ríkisstjórnin skal ávallt bera meiri háttar utanríkismál fyrir fram undir nefndina' (The government shall always bring important foreign affairs matters before the committee in advance). - **SOV-LEGAL-020** (líkindi: 0.519)

- **EEA-LEGAL-021** (líkindi: 0.506)
  The European Commission's official enlargement materials explicitly state that accession negotiations are about 'the conditions and timing for the candidate's adoption, implementation and enforcement of all current EU rules (the acquis)' — not about whether to adopt the rules. The Commission's factsheet 'EU Accession Process: Steps towards Joining' (updated 2022) makes clear that 'the candidate accepts the acquis as it stands at the time of accession.' The EU Council's negotiating frameworks for all current candidates contain the standard clause to this effect. The commonly cited figure that '95% of the acquis is non-negotiable' reflects the distinction between the rules themselves (non-negotiable) and the implementation timeline (negotiable). In practice, candidates can negotiate: transition periods (time-limited derogations, typically 3–12 years); technical adaptations (translating institutional references); and financial arrangements (budget contributions, access to structural funds). This framework applied to Iceland's 2010–2013 negotiations and would apply to any resumed negotiations.
  *Heimild: European Commission — 'EU Accession Process: Steps towards Joining'; Council negotiating frameworks*
  Fyrirvarar: While technically accurate, this framing can understate the genuine flexibility within transition arrangements. Poland's 12-year land purchase derogation, for example, was a significant concession. Some legal scholars argue that Article 49 TEU technically allows any negotiated outcome. The political distinction between 'negotiating the rules' and 'negotiating when to apply the rules' is real but can be overdrawn — a sufficiently long transition period may function similarly to an exemption in practice.
