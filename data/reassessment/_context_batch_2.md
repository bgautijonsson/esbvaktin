# Endurmat fullyrðinga — Lota 2

Þú ert staðreyndaprófari fyrir ESBvaktin.is, óháðan vettvang um
þjóðaratkvæðagreiðslu Íslands um ESB-aðild (29. ágúst 2026).
Þú metur fullyrðingar jafnt hvort sem þær eru ESB-jákvæðar eða ESB-neikvæðar.

Þessar fullyrðingar voru áður flokkaðar sem **óstaðfestanlegar** (unverifiable)
vegna þess að heimildir skorti. Nú eru nýjar heimildir komnar í gagnagrunninn.
Endurmettu hverja fullyrðingu í ljósi heimildanna hér að neðan.

## Fullyrðing 1 (claim_id: 114)

**Íslenskur texti:** Höfundur greinarinnar sé þingmaður Sjálfstæðisflokksins.
**Enskur texti:** The author is an MP for the Independence Party (Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn).
**Flokkur:** party_positions

**Heimildir úr staðreyndagrunni:**

- **POL-DATA-011** (líkindi: 0.56)
  The EU question cuts across traditional party lines in the Althingi. An informal cross-party pro-EU grouping includes all Viðreisn MPs (7), most Samfylkingin MPs (6 of 7), approximately half of Píratar MPs (2 of 4), and individual MPs from Left-Greens and even the Independence Party — totalling approximately 18–22 of 63 MPs who would personally favour EU membership. The anti-EU bloc is larger, comprising most Independence Party MPs (12 of 16), all Progressive Party MPs (8), all Miðflokkur MPs (5), all Flokkur fólksins MPs (3), and some Left-Green MPs — approximately 32–36 MPs. The remaining 5–13 MPs are genuinely uncommitted or follow their party's neutral stance. These numbers are estimates based on public statements and voting patterns rather than a formal count.
  *Heimild: Althingi — Public statements and committee records; media analysis of MP positions*
  Fyrirvarar: MP-level counts are approximate and based on public statements, which may not reflect private views. The Althingi's composition may change before the referendum due to party switches or by-elections. The referendum result is legally binding regardless of the parliamentary balance.

- **POLITICAL-DATA-006** (líkindi: 0.557)
  Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn (Independence Party) holds 13 Althingi seats in session 157, tied with Samfylkingin as the largest party. The party is historically EU-sceptic, emphasising national sovereignty and independent fisheries management. Current MPs include: Diljá Mist Einarsdóttir (Reykjavíkurkjördæmi norður), Guðlaugur Þór Þórðarson (Reykjavíkurkjördæmi norður), Þórdís Kolbrún Reykfjörð Gylfadóttir (Suðvesturkjördæmi), Bryndís Haraldsdóttir (Suðvesturkjördæmi), Hildur Sverrisdóttir (Reykjavíkurkjördæmi suður), Jón Gunnarsson (Suðvesturkjördæmi), Jón Pétur Zimsen (Reykjavíkurkjördæmi suður), Guðrún Hafsteinsdóttir (Suðurkjördæmi), Vilhjálmur Árnason (Suðurkjördæmi), Njáll Trausti Friðbertsson (Norðausturkjördæmi), Jens Garðar Helgason (Norðausturkjördæmi), Rósa Guðbjartsdóttir (Suðvesturkjördæmi), Ólafur Adolfsson (Norðvesturkjördæmi).
  *Heimild: Althingi — Session 157 member registry*
  Fyrirvarar: The Independence Party has internal factions with varying views on European integration. Some members support EEA membership but oppose full EU accession. Guðlaugur Þór Þórðarson served as Foreign Minister (2017–2021) and is considered more pragmatic on EU issues.

- **POLL-DATA-011** (líkindi: 0.54)
  EU support varies dramatically by party affiliation. Among Viðreisn voters, ~90% support EU membership; among Samfylkingin voters, ~75%. Píratar voters show ~55% support, and Left-Green voters ~45%. On the opposing side, Sjálfstæðisflokkur voters show ~25% support, Framsóknarflokkur voters ~20%, Miðflokkur voters ~10%, and Flokkur fólksins voters ~10%. These figures (from late 2025 polling) suggest that the referendum will cut across party lines, with significant minorities within each party diverging from the official party position. The ~25% of Independence Party voters who support membership is particularly significant given that party's large vote share (~22% in recent elections).
  *Heimild: Gallup Ísland — EU support by party affiliation (November 2025)*
  Fyrirvarar: Party-based subsamples have larger margins of error. Voters may shift party allegiance before the referendum, particularly if parties take stronger stances. The gap between party leadership positions and voter preferences suggests the referendum outcome will not simply reflect the parliamentary balance of power.

- **POL-DATA-009** (líkindi: 0.54)
  Píratar (Pirate Party) take a process-oriented rather than position-oriented stance on EU membership. The party strongly supported the referendum as an exercise in direct democracy and has advocated for maximum transparency in the pre-referendum information campaign. On substance, the party's EU-relevant positions are mixed: they support the digital single market and free internet governance (aligning with EU initiatives), but oppose EU intellectual property frameworks (particularly software patents and strict copyright enforcement) and are sceptical of EU surveillance and data retention measures. Individual Pirate MPs lean approximately 60–40 towards supporting membership. The party's voter base (predominantly young, urban, tech-savvy) shows approximately 55% EU support.
  *Heimild: Píratar — Policy platform; Althingi committee contributions on EU matters*
  Fyrirvarar: The Pirate Party's influence has waned from its peak (14.5% in 2016 elections), and its current parliamentary representation is smaller. The party's decentralised decision-making structure means that official positions may not reflect the views of all members or voters.

- **POL-DATA-020** (líkindi: 0.521)
  As of March 2026, the main opposition parties' positions on the EU referendum are: Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn (Independence Party, 13 seats) opposes EU membership but has not called for boycotting the referendum. The party's official position emphasises sovereignty, control of natural resources (especially fisheries), and the adequacy of the EEA arrangement. Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn has criticised the 29 August referendum date as unsuitable, arguing meaningful public debate cannot occur during summer holidays. The party's former leader submitted parliamentary resolutions six times to withdraw Iceland's EU application entirely. Miðflokkurinn (Centre Party, 8 seats) is firmly opposed to EU membership and has been the most vocally anti-EU party in Alþingi, consistently arguing against both membership and the referendum process. Framsóknarflokkurinn (Progressive Party, 4 seats) is internally divided but historically EU-sceptic; approximately half of its voters oppose continuing negotiations.
  *Heimild: Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn — party website (xd.is); Miðflokkurinn — public statements; Gallup Þjóðarpúls party breakdown March 2026*
  Fyrirvarar: Party positions can shift, especially as the referendum approaches and the campaign intensifies. Internal divisions exist within all parties — Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn has a pro-business wing that is more open to EU membership, and individual MPs may break with party lines. The 4-seat Framsóknarflokkurinn is diminished from its historical strength and its position carries less weight than in previous decades.

- **POLITICAL-DATA-005** (líkindi: 0.491)
  Miðflokkurinn (Centre Party) holds 8 Althingi seats in session 157. The party was founded in 2017 by Sigmundur Davíð Gunnlaugsson, former Prime Minister (2013–2016), who remains a sitting MP for Norðausturkjördæmi. The party takes a strong anti-EU position, opposing both EU membership and further EEA integration. Current MPs: Bergþór Ólason (Suðvesturkjördæmi), Sigmundur Davíð Gunnlaugsson (Norðausturkjördæmi), Sigríður Á. Andersen (Reykjavíkurkjördæmi norður), Karl Gauti Hjaltason (Suðurkjördæmi), Nanna Margrét Gunnlaugsdóttir (Suðvesturkjördæmi), Ingibjörg Davíðsdóttir (Norðvesturkjördæmi), Snorri Másson (Reykjavíkurkjördæmi suður), Þorgrímur Sigmundsson (Norðausturkjördæmi).
  *Heimild: Althingi — Session 157 member registry*
  Fyrirvarar: Party membership and MP count may change. Sigmundur Davíð resigned as party chair but remains an MP.

- **POLITICAL-DATA-004** (líkindi: 0.49)
  Viðreisn (Reform Party) is led by Þorgerður Katrín Gunnarsdóttir, who serves as utanríkisráðherra (Foreign Minister) in the current government. She is an MP for Suðvesturkjördæmi. Viðreisn was founded in 2014 (originally as a political movement, became a party in 2016) with a pro-EU platform as a core policy. The party holds 11 Althingi seats and consistently advocates for completing EU accession negotiations. Other prominent Viðreisn MPs include Pawel Bartoszek (Reykjavíkurkjördæmi norður), Jón Gnarr (Reykjavíkurkjördæmi suður), and Hanna Katrín Friðriksson (Reykjavíkurkjördæmi norður, also Minister of Industries).
  *Heimild: Althingi — Session 157; Viðreisn party platform*
  Fyrirvarar: Party leadership may change at party congresses. Individual MP views may not fully align with the party platform.

- **POLITICAL-DATA-007** (líkindi: 0.482)
  Framsóknarflokkurinn (Progressive Party) holds 4 Althingi seats in session 157, its lowest representation in modern history. The party traditionally represents rural interests and opposes EU membership primarily over agricultural and fisheries policy concerns. Current MPs: Halla Hrund Logadóttir (Suðurkjördæmi), Ingibjörg Isaksen (Norðausturkjördæmi), Stefán Vagn Stefánsson (Norðvesturkjördæmi), Þórarinn Ingi Pétursson (Norðausturkjördæmi).
  *Heimild: Althingi — Session 157 member registry*
  Fyrirvarar: The Progressive Party's reduced size may limit its influence in the EU debate compared to previous terms. Some members have indicated openness to revisiting the party's EU stance.

## Fullyrðing 2 (claim_id: 102)

**Íslenskur texti:** Össur Skarphéðinsson hafi verið utanríkisráðherra Íslands þegar blaðamannafundurinn með Stefan Füle var haldinn.
**Enskur texti:** Össur Skarphéðinsson was Foreign Minister of Iceland at the time of the press conference with Stefan Füle
**Flokkur:** party_positions

**Heimildir úr staðreyndagrunni:**

- **PARTY-DATA-012** (líkindi: 0.518)
  Miðflokkurinn (Centre Party), led by Sigmundur Davíð Gunnlaugsson, is aligned with the European Conservatives and Reformists (ECR) grouping and is strongly opposed to EU integration. In the context of the 2026 EU referendum campaign, Norwegian outlet Altinget.no reported that Miðflokkurinn representatives had met with US Republican Party contacts, and that a political advisor linked to Nigel Farage visited Iceland twice in early 2026. Heimildin also reported on these international connections. Sigmundur Davíð has cultivated ties with international conservative and Eurosceptic movements since founding the party in 2017 after splitting from the Progressive Party. The party holds 8 seats in Althingi as of session 157.
  *Heimild: Altinget.no; Heimildin; Europe Elects party classification*
  Fyrirvarar: The specific nature and extent of Miðflokkurinn's international contacts are based on media reporting and have not been independently verified in full. The party has not published a formal statement confirming or denying all reported meetings. The ECR alignment is informal as Iceland is not an EU member.

- **POLITICAL-DATA-002** (líkindi: 0.515)
  The current Icelandic government is a coalition of Samfylkingin (Social Democrats, 13 seats), Viðreisn (Reform Party, 11 seats), and Flokkur fólksins (People's Party, 8 seats), holding 32 of 63 Althingi seats. The Prime Minister is Kristrún Frostadóttir (Samfylkingin). This is the first government coalition where both explicitly pro-EU parties (Samfylkingin and Viðreisn) serve together, which has been cited as a factor in advancing the EU referendum.
  *Heimild: Althingi — Government composition, session 157*
  Fyrirvarar: Flokkur fólksins is EU-sceptic despite being in coalition with two pro-EU parties. The government's agreement on a referendum does not mean all coalition parties support EU membership.

- **PARTY-DATA-013** (líkindi: 0.506)
  The coalition government formed in late 2024, comprising Samfylkingin (Social Democrats, 13 seats), Viðreisn (Reform Party, 11 seats), and Flokkur fólksins (People's Party, 8 seats), agreed in their coalition agreement to hold a referendum on whether to resume EU accession negotiations by 2027. The referendum was subsequently scheduled for 29 August 2026. The question asks whether Iceland should resume accession negotiations with the EU — not whether to join. This is the first Icelandic government since Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir's government (2009–2013) to actively pursue EU-related policy. Prime Minister Kristrún Frostadóttir (Samfylkingin) and Foreign Minister Þorgerður Katrín Gunnarsdóttir (Viðreisn) have been the primary government figures advancing the referendum. Previous governments, including those led by Sigmundur Davíð Gunnlaugsson (2013–2016) and Katrín Jakobsdóttir (2017–2024), did not pursue EU accession despite intermittent public calls for a referendum.
  *Heimild: Government of Iceland; Euronews; France24; coalition agreement*
  Fyrirvarar: Flokkur fólksins is EU-sceptic despite being in the coalition. Their agreement to hold a referendum does not imply support for EU membership or resuming negotiations. The coalition holds a slim five-seat majority (32 of 63 seats).

- **POLITICAL-DATA-013** (líkindi: 0.498)
  Miðflokkurinn (Centre Party), founded in 2017 by Sigmundur Davíð Gunnlaugsson, is affiliated with the European Conservatives and Reformists (ECR) group at the European level, which includes parties such as the Italian Fratelli d'Italia (led by PM Giorgia Meloni), the Polish Law and Justice party (PiS), and the Sweden Democrats. The party has positioned itself as Eurosceptic and sovereigntist. Reports in Icelandic media have noted contacts between Miðflokkurinn representatives and US Republican Party circles, though the nature and extent of these contacts are not fully documented in public records. Sigmundur Davíð has characterised the government's EU referendum as 'Evrópubrölt' (roughly: 'EU agitation/hustle') and accused the government of concealing its pro-EU agenda from the public and from Althingi.
  *Heimild: Miðflokkurinn — Party website and statements; ECR Party membership; Icelandic media reports*
  Fyrirvarar: Political party connections across borders are normal in democratic politics — most Icelandic parties have international affiliations. The characterisation of contacts with the US Republican Party comes primarily from political opponents and media reports, and the significance of any such contacts is disputed. Sigmundur Davíð's 'Evrópubrölt' characterisation is a political opinion, not a factual claim.

- **POLITICAL-DATA-003** (líkindi: 0.484)
  The cabinet of session 157 comprises ministers from the three coalition parties. Samfylkingin: Kristrún Frostadóttir (forsætisráðherra / Prime Minister), Alma D. Möller (heilbrigðisráðherra / Minister of Health), Jóhann Páll Jóhannsson (umhverfis-, orku- og loftslagsráðherra / Minister of Environment, Energy and Climate), Logi Einarsson (menningar-, nýsköpunar- og háskólaráðherra / Minister of Culture, Innovation and Higher Education; also ráðherra norrænna samstarfsmála / Minister for Nordic Cooperation). Viðreisn: Þorgerður Katrín Gunnarsdóttir (utanríkisráðherra / Foreign Minister), Daði Már Kristófersson (fjármála- og efnahagsráðherra / Minister of Finance and Economic Affairs), Hanna Katrín Friðriksson (atvinnuvegaráðherra / Minister of Industries), Þorbjörg Sigríður Gunnlaugsdóttir (dómsmálaráðherra / Minister of Justice). Flokkur fólksins: Eyjólfur Ármannsson (innviðaráðherra / Minister of Infrastructure), Inga Sæland (félags- og húsnæðismálaráðherra / Minister of Social Affairs and Housing), Guðmundur Ingi Kristinsson (mennta- og barnamálaráðherra / Minister of Education and Children), Ragnar Þór Ingólfsson (félags- og húsnæðismálaráðherra / Minister of Social Affairs and Housing).
  *Heimild: Althingi — Ministers registry, session 157*
  Fyrirvarar: Cabinet composition may change with reshuffles. Some ministers hold dual portfolios. Daði Már Kristófersson and Guðmundur Ingi Kristinsson are not sitting MPs (outside ministers).

- **POLITICAL-DATA-010** (líkindi: 0.459)
  Iceland's previous EU accession negotiations were conducted from 2010 to 2013. Össur Skarphéðinsson (Samfylkingin) served as Foreign Minister from 2009 to 2013 and led the accession process. The application was submitted on 16 July 2009 under PM Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir's government (Samfylkingin–VG coalition). Negotiations progressed through 11 of 33 chapters before the incoming Sjálfstæðisflokkur–Framsóknarflokkur government under PM Sigmundur Davíð Gunnlaugsson effectively froze the process in 2013. Iceland formally withdrew its application on 12 March 2015 under Foreign Minister Gunnar Bragi Sveinsson (Framsóknarflokkur).
  *Heimild: Althingi — Historical records; European Commission accession records*
  Fyrirvarar: The legal status of the 2015 withdrawal has been debated, as Althingi did not formally vote on withdrawing the application — the government acted unilaterally. Some argue the original application remains technically open. The current government's approach is to hold a new referendum rather than resume the old application.
