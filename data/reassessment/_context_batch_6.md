## Fullyrðing 1 (claim_id: 63)

**Íslenskur texti:** Þjóðaratkvæðagreiðsla er betra og skilvirkara samráð en samráð við utanríkismálanefnd, að mati utanríkisráðherra.
**Flokkur:** sovereignty

**Heimildir úr staðreyndagrunni:**

- **SOV-LEGAL-019** (líkindi: 0.538)
  Article 24 of the Icelandic Parliamentary Rules of Procedure (lög um þingsköp Alþingis, nr. 55/1991, with subsequent amendments) establishes that the government shall always consult the Althingi Foreign Affairs Committee (utanríkismálanefnd) on important foreign affairs matters before making decisions. The article states: 'Ríkisstjórnin skal ávallt bera meiri háttar utanríkismál fyrir fram undir nefndina' (The government shall always bring important foreign affairs matters before the committee in advance). The committee consists of members from all parliamentary parties and serves as the primary parliamentary oversight mechanism for foreign policy. The provision is designed to prevent unilateral executive decisions on foreign affairs without parliamentary consultation. Whether a specific matter qualifies as 'meiri háttar utanríkismál' (important foreign affairs matter) is subject to interpretation, and disagreements between government and opposition on this classification have been a recurring feature of Icelandic parliamentary politics.
  *Heimild: Lög um þingsköp Alþingis nr. 55/1991; Alþingi official website*
  Fyrirvarar: The article establishes a consultation requirement, not a veto power for the committee. The government retains decision-making authority on foreign affairs — the committee must be consulted but its approval is not legally required. Whether submitting a parliamentary resolution (þingsályktunartillaga) to hold a referendum constitutes an 'important foreign affairs matter' requiring prior committee consultation is a matter of legal interpretation on which reasonable lawyers disagree.

- **POLL-DATA-010** (líkindi: 0.482)
  The wording of EU-related poll questions significantly affects measured support. When asked 'Should Iceland apply for EU membership?', support is typically 3–5 percentage points higher than when asked 'Should Iceland join the EU?', and 5–8 points higher than when asked 'Are you in favour of EU membership?'. The application question implies a process with future decision points, while the membership question implies finality. The binding referendum question — 'Viljið þér að Ísland gerist aðili að Evrópusambandinu?' ('Do you want Iceland to become a member of the European Union?') — uses the direct membership framing, which historically produces lower support levels. Pollsters using different wordings have produced divergent results on the same day by up to 10 percentage points.
  *Heimild: Félagsvísindastofnun — Methodology notes on EU polling; comparison of Gallup, MMR, and Maskína polls*
  Fyrirvarar: Question wording effects are well-documented in referendum polling internationally. The official referendum question was set by legislation and cannot be changed. Pre-referendum polls using different wording should be interpreted with caution when predicting the actual vote.

- **POLL-DATA-020** (líkindi: 0.481)
  The Gallup Þjóðarpúls survey (19 February – 4 March 2026, n≈810) found that a majority of Icelandic respondents supported holding a national referendum on resuming EU accession negotiations. According to the published results, roughly 60% of respondents supported the referendum, approximately 30% opposed it, and approximately 10% were undecided or did not respond. The survey found sharp party-based divisions: voters of Viðreisn (Reform Party) and Samfylkingin (Social Democrats) were the most strongly supportive, while voters of Miðflokkurinn (Centre Party) and Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn (Independence Party) were the most strongly opposed. The survey measured support for holding a referendum on resuming negotiations — not support for EU membership itself, which typically polls lower. This distinction is important: a voter may support giving people the chance to vote on the question while personally opposing EU membership.
  *Heimild: Gallup hf. — Þjóðarpúls; Vísir*
  Fyrirvarar: Pre-referendum polling can diverge significantly from actual voting outcomes. Iceland's small population means that campaign dynamics, events, and high-profile endorsements can shift opinion rapidly. The distinction between 'support for a referendum' and 'support for EU membership' is crucial — historically, support for holding a vote runs higher than support for the substantive position. Poll results are also sensitive to question wording and ordering effects.

- **SOV-LEGAL-013** (líkindi: 0.471)
  Under the EEA Agreement, the EFTA Court has jurisdiction over EEA law in EFTA states, while the Court of Justice of the EU (CJEU) has jurisdiction in EU member states. The EFTA Court was designed to mirror the CJEU to ensure homogeneity of EEA law, and in practice follows CJEU case law closely. However, the EFTA Court's rulings are advisory rather than binding on national courts (unlike CJEU rulings, which have direct effect and supremacy). The EFTA Court has three judges (one per EEA EFTA state) and handles approximately 10–15 cases per year, compared to the CJEU's ~1,600 cases. EU membership would replace EFTA Court jurisdiction with the CJEU, bringing binding preliminary rulings, direct effect of EU law, and state liability for breaches.
  *Heimild: EEA Agreement, Article 108; EFTA Court — Annual Report 2024; CJEU — Annual Report 2024*
  Fyrirvarar: In practice, the distinction between the EFTA Court and CJEU is less significant than it appears — Icelandic courts generally follow EFTA Court advisory opinions, and the EFTA Court follows CJEU jurisprudence. The more significant change from EU membership would be the principles of direct effect and supremacy of EU law, which do not formally apply under the EEA.

## Fullyrðing 2 (claim_id: 52)

- **SOV-DATA-006** (líkindi: 0.663)
  The Icelandic government announced in early 2026 that a national referendum on resuming EU accession negotiations would be held on 29 August 2026. The referendum is based on a parliamentary resolution (þingsályktunartillaga) submitted by the Foreign Minister. The three coalition parties — Samfylkingin (Social Democrats), Viðreisn (Reform Party), and Flokkur fólksins (People's Party) — support holding the referendum. The referendum question concerns whether Iceland should resume the accession negotiations that were suspended in 2013, not whether Iceland should join the EU outright. This is an advisory referendum (ráðgefandi þjóðaratkvæðagreiðsla), as Icelandic constitutional law does not provide for binding referenda on policy questions (the only binding referendum mechanism is under Article 26 for presidential vetoes of legislation).
  *Heimild: Government of Iceland announcement; Althingi parliamentary records*
  Fyrirvarar: The referendum is advisory, meaning the Althingi is not legally bound by the result. However, Icelandic political convention gives strong weight to referendum outcomes — the 2012 constitutional referendum's non-implementation is considered an exception that has been widely criticised. The date of 29 August was chosen by the government and has been criticised by opposition parties as too soon for adequate public debate.

- **POLL-DATA-001** (líkindi: 0.619)
  Gallup polls conducted in Iceland between 2022 and early 2026 show a gradual shift in public opinion on EU membership. Support for applying for EU membership rose from approximately 30% in 2022 to around 45% by late 2025, while opposition declined from roughly 50% to 40%. The remainder were undecided. The trend accelerated following the Althingi's decision in 2024 to hold a binding referendum.
  *Heimild: Gallup Ísland — EU membership polling*
  Fyrirvarar: Polling methodology and question wording vary between surveys. Some polls ask about 'applying for membership' while others ask about 'joining the EU', which can produce different results. Undecided voters remain a large and potentially decisive group.

- **POLL-DATA-007** (líkindi: 0.595)
  The Gallup Þjóðarpúls survey on the EU referendum was conducted online from 19 February to 4 March 2026 with a total sample size of 1,898 respondents randomly selected from Gallup's opinion panel. The response rate was 42.7%. The survey asked two main questions: whether respondents support holding a referendum on continuing EU accession negotiations, and how they would likely vote. Key headline findings: 57% support holding the referendum, approximately 30% oppose, and roughly 12% are neutral or undecided. On voting intention if a referendum were held: support for resuming negotiations leads, but when asked about actual EU membership (as opposed to negotiations), the split is closer to 42%–42% with 16% undecided. The gap between support for negotiations and support for membership is a significant methodological distinction in interpreting the results.
  *Heimild: Gallup Iceland / Þjóðarpúls*
  Fyrirvarar: The 42.7% response rate is reasonable for an online panel survey but introduces potential non-response bias. Online panel surveys may under-represent older and rural populations. The distinction between support for holding a referendum, support for resuming negotiations, and support for EU membership yields substantially different numbers and must be carefully distinguished.

- **PARTY-DATA-011** (líkindi: 0.51)
  After the 2013 Icelandic parliamentary elections, the new coalition government of the Progressive Party and Independence Party sought to withdraw Iceland's EU membership application. In February 2015, the governing parties submitted a bill to Althingi seeking approval to formally withdraw, but the bill failed due to vigorous opposition obstruction and was never put to a final vote. The EU's response was ambiguous — the European Commission's spokesperson stated that Sveinsson's letter did not constitute a formal withdrawal of the application. The Latvian EU presidency replied on behalf of the Council. The decision to bypass parliament provoked significant public protests outside the Althingi.   Fyrirvarar: The legal status of Iceland's application remains disputed.
