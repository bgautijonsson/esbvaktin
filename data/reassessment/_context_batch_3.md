# Endurmat fullyrðinga — Lota 3

Þú ert staðreyndaprófari fyrir ESBvaktin.is, óháðan vettvang um
þjóðaratkvæðagreiðslu Íslands um ESB-aðild (29. ágúst 2026).
Þú metur fullyrðingar jafnt hvort sem þær eru ESB-jákvæðar eða ESB-neikvæðar.

Þessar fullyrðingar voru áður flokkaðar sem **óstaðfestanlegar** (unverifiable)
vegna þess að heimildir skorti. Nú eru nýjar heimildir komnar í gagnagrunninn.
Endurmettu hverja fullyrðingu í ljósi heimildanna hér að neðan.

## Fullyrðing 1 (claim_id: 152)

**Íslenskur texti:** Ríkisstjórn Donalds Trump vill halda Íslandi utan Evrópusambandsins vegna þess að innganga myndi veikja samningsstöðu Bandaríkjanna gagnvart Íslandi í viðskiptum og skaða varnarmál Bandaríkjanna.
**Flokkur:** sovereignty

**Heimildir úr staðreyndagrunni:**

- **SOV-DATA-008** (líkindi: 0.556)
  The United States has historically maintained strategic interests in Iceland due to Iceland's location in the North Atlantic (controlling the GIUK gap — Greenland-Iceland-UK passage — which is critical for monitoring submarine activity) and through the 1951 bilateral defence agreement. The US maintained a permanent military presence at Naval Air Station Keflavík from 1951 until its closure in 2006. After the closure, bilateral defence cooperation continued through periodic deployments and the 2016 Iceland-US Security Cooperation Agreement, which provided US funding for upgrading military infrastructure at Keflavík. Some analysts and political commentators have argued that the US may prefer Iceland to remain outside the EU to maintain uncomplicated bilateral defence and trade relations. However, there is no documented evidence of direct US government opposition to Icelandic EU membership — the official US position has been that EU membership is Iceland's sovereign decision. The US has close EU allies (France, Germany, the Netherlands) and also has NATO allies outside the EU (Norway, Turkey), suggesting that EU membership status does not inherently conflict with US alliance interests.
  *Heimild: 1951 US-Iceland Defence Agreement; 2016 Iceland-US Security Cooperation Agreement; US Department of State bilateral relations factsheet*
  Fyrirvarar: Geopolitical analysis of US preferences regarding Iceland's EU membership is speculative. The US has publicly stated it is Iceland's sovereign choice. Some Icelandic commentators from both pro- and anti-EU perspectives have projected US preferences onto the debate without citing direct evidence. The strategic calculus may have shifted since Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine, which has made transatlantic unity (including EU-NATO cooperation) a higher US priority.

- **SOV-DATA-001** (líkindi: 0.52)
  Iceland is a member of the Arctic Council and chairs the Council of Europe (distinct from the EU). The EU has sought observer status in the Arctic Council since 2008 but has been repeatedly blocked, most recently by Russia. Three EU member states (Denmark/Greenland, Finland, Sweden) are Arctic Council members. The EU's Arctic policy, updated in 2021, emphasises climate change, sustainable development, and international cooperation. EU membership would not diminish Iceland's own Arctic Council seat but would add an EU coordination layer to Iceland's Arctic policy positions.
  *Heimild: European Commission — EU Arctic Policy, Joint Communication JOIN(2021) 27*
  Fyrirvarar: Arctic geopolitics have shifted significantly since Russia's suspension from Arctic Council cooperation in 2022. Iceland's strategic position in the North Atlantic (GIUK gap) has gained renewed military significance. EU membership could strengthen or complicate Iceland's independent Arctic voice depending on alignment of interests.

- **POL-DATA-019** (líkindi: 0.509)
  Bændasamtök Íslands (the Icelandic Farmers Association, BÍ) is officially opposed to Iceland's EU membership, a position maintained consistently since the 2009 membership application. BÍ argues that EU membership and the Common Agricultural Policy would undermine Icelandic agriculture by removing tariff protection (30–100%+ on dairy, meat, and key products), exposing small-scale Icelandic farms to competition from larger, lower-cost EU producers, and replacing Iceland's tailored subsidy system with CAP payments that may not adequately support production in harsh subarctic conditions. However, BÍ has also taken the pragmatic step of formulating minimum requirements (lágmarkskröfur) for Icelandic agriculture in any EU accession scenario, calling on the government to ensure that EU membership would not undermine the operational conditions and investments of active farmers. Internal divisions exist: farmer meetings (bændafundir) held around Iceland have revealed that not all farmers share the association leadership's certainty that opposition is the right position.
  *Heimild: Bændasamtök Íslands — public statements and position papers (2009–2026); Vísir reporting*
  Fyrirvarar: BÍ's position represents the institutional stance of the association's leadership, not necessarily all individual farmers. The 'minimum requirements' approach suggests tacit acknowledgment that membership remains possible and preparations should be made. BÍ is one of Iceland's most influential interest groups and has significant lobbying power, particularly through Framsóknarflokkurinn (Progressive Party), historically the farmers' party.

- **SOV-LEGAL-014** (líkindi: 0.506)
  EU membership would bring Iceland into the Common Foreign and Security Policy (CFSP) and Common Security and Defence Policy (CSDP). Iceland currently cooperates with NATO (member since 1949, founding member) and has bilateral defence agreements with the United States, Denmark, and Norway. Iceland has no military forces but maintains a Coast Guard. Under CFSP, Iceland would be expected to align with EU foreign policy positions, including sanctions regimes. In practice, Iceland already voluntarily aligns with approximately 70–80% of EU CFSP declarations and sanctions. The more significant change would be participation in EU defence initiatives (PESCO, European Defence Fund), though these remain voluntary for member states.
  *Heimild: TEU, Title V — CFSP provisions; Icelandic Ministry for Foreign Affairs — Annual alignment reports*
  Fyrirvarar: CFSP decisions require unanimity in the Council, so Iceland could veto any foreign policy position it disagreed with. EU defence cooperation is voluntary — Ireland, Austria, and Malta demonstrate that military neutrality is compatible with EU membership. Iceland's NATO membership would continue to be its primary security framework.

- **SOV-HIST-003** (líkindi: 0.503)
  Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 significantly increased Iceland's geopolitical importance. NATO allies resumed regular air policing deployments to Keflavík, and in March 2022 the Icelandic government considered whether continuous air policing was needed rather than periodic rotations. The EU adopted the Strategic Compass in March 2022, establishing a framework for EU security and defence policy through 2030, including a 5,000-strong EU Rapid Deployment Capacity. The Strategic Compass explicitly states that EU defence is 'complementary to NATO', but the EU's growing defence ambitions — including a proposed EUR 150 billion European defence spending plan (2025) — create potential overlap with NATO structures. For Iceland, EU membership would add a CSDP layer to its existing NATO-centred security architecture, potentially complicating the bilateral US-Iceland defence relationship and Iceland's unique position as a NATO member without armed forces.
  *Heimild: EU Strategic Compass for Security and Defence (March 2022); NATO Allied Air Command — Icelandic Air Policing; IISS Strategic Survey 2023*
  Fyrirvarar: The interaction between EU defence policy and NATO is evolving and uncertain. Twenty-three EU member states are also NATO members, and institutional overlap is managed pragmatically. Iceland's lack of armed forces makes CSDP participation largely symbolic. The claim that EU membership would undermine the US-Iceland bilateral relationship is speculative — many NATO allies are also EU members without conflict.

- **AGRI-DATA-008** (líkindi: 0.496)
  Under the EEA Agreement, agriculture is largely excluded from free movement of goods. EEA Article 19 provides for periodic reviews to progressively liberalise agricultural trade, but Iceland and Norway have maintained very high tariffs on key products: Iceland applies tariffs of 30–100%+ on dairy, meat, and some vegetables. Under full EU membership, all internal tariffs would be eliminated, and Iceland would adopt the EU's Common External Tariff. This would expose Icelandic dairy and lamb producers to competition from EU producers with larger scale and lower costs. However, Iceland would also gain tariff-free access to the EU's 450-million consumer market for its agricultural exports (currently minimal). The key difference: EEA allows Iceland to protect its agricultural market; EU membership would not, except through negotiated transition periods and possible national aid provisions.
  *Heimild: EEA Agreement Article 19; Icelandic Ministry for Foreign Affairs — EEA review documentation*
  Fyrirvarar: The practical impact depends heavily on transition periods and any special provisions Iceland might negotiate. Iceland's high production costs (small scale, harsh climate, expensive labour) mean that even with full market access, Icelandic agricultural exports would remain small. The main impact would be on import competition, not export opportunities.

- **POL-DATA-017** (líkindi: 0.495)
  Viðskiptaráð Íslands (Iceland Chamber of Commerce) has taken a pro-EU position, emphasising competitiveness and business environment arguments. The Chamber's 2025 report estimates that EU membership would boost Iceland's GDP by 2–4% over 10 years through reduced trade costs, lower interest rates, and increased FDI. The Chamber highlights that Iceland ranks poorly on the World Bank's trading-across-borders indicators compared to EU/EEA peers, partly due to customs procedures and regulatory divergences that persist despite EEA membership. The Chamber also argues that the EEA's 'decision-shaping' deficit means Icelandic businesses must comply with EU regulations without having influenced their design.
  *Heimild: Viðskiptaráð Íslands — 'The Economic Case for EU Membership' (2025)*
  Fyrirvarar: GDP growth estimates from membership are model-dependent and vary widely. The Chamber's assessment may overweight the benefits to internationally oriented firms while underweighting adjustment costs for domestically focused sectors. Similar GDP boost estimates for other accession countries have sometimes proved optimistic.

- **PARTY-DATA-011** (líkindi: 0.495)
  After the 2013 Icelandic parliamentary elections, the new coalition government of the Progressive Party and Independence Party sought to withdraw Iceland's EU membership application. In February 2015, the governing parties submitted a bill to Althingi seeking approval to formally withdraw, but the bill failed due to vigorous opposition obstruction and was never put to a final vote. Foreign Minister Gunnar Bragi Sveinsson then unilaterally sent a letter to the EU on 12 March 2015, stating that Iceland should no longer be regarded as a candidate country. The EU's response was ambiguous — the European Commission's spokesperson stated that Sveinsson's letter did not constitute a formal withdrawal of the application. The Latvian EU presidency replied on behalf of the Council. The decision to bypass parliament provoked significant public protests outside the Althingi. The EU has continued to regard Iceland's 2009 application as technically still valid, a point that became relevant again in the lead-up to the 2026 referendum.
  *Heimild: Icelandic Ministry for Foreign Affairs; EU Council of Ministers; Reykjavik Grapevine; IceNews*
  Fyrirvarar: The legal status of Iceland's application remains disputed. The EU has never formally acknowledged the withdrawal, and the European Commission stated in March 2026 that the application is still valid. Icelandic legal scholars disagree on whether a foreign minister's letter can override a parliamentary process.

## Fullyrðing 2 (claim_id: 109)

**Íslenskur texti:** Þingmenn Sjálfstæðisflokksins hafi lagt fram frumvarp um reglur um starfsval eftir ákveðin opinber embætti, þar sem lagt sé til 18 mánaða biðtími áður en embættismenn sem tóku þátt í aðildarviðræðum geti tekið við stöðum hjá gagnaðilanum.
**Enskur texti:** The Independence Party MPs have submitted a bill on rules governing career choices after certain public offices, proposing an 18-month cooling-off period before officials involved in accession negotiations can take positions with the negotiating counterpart.
**Flokkur:** sovereignty

**Heimildir úr staðreyndagrunni:**

- **SOV-LEGAL-007** (líkindi: 0.617)
  Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn (Independence Party) introduced a bill in Althingi proposing an 18-month cooling-off period that would prevent senior officials and ministerial advisors involved in negotiations with international organisations from accepting positions with those organisations after leaving office. The bill was introduced in the context of potential EU accession negotiations, framed as a safeguard against conflicts of interest. Similar 'revolving door' regulations exist at the EU institutional level: EU Commissioners face an 18-month cooling-off period (extended to 24 months for the Commission President), and the European Ombudsman has called for stricter enforcement. Several EU member states, including France and Germany, have analogous rules for senior civil servants.
  *Heimild: Alþingi bill database; EU Staff Regulations Art. 16; European Ombudsman recommendations*
  Fyrirvarar: The bill's exact status in Althingi (whether it has been debated, referred to committee, or voted on) requires verification from the Althingi bill database. The comparison with EU-level cooling-off periods is contextual — the Icelandic bill's scope and enforcement mechanisms may differ substantially.

- **SOV-LEGAL-001** (líkindi: 0.562)
  Article 50 of the Treaty on European Union (TEU) provides that any member state may decide to withdraw from the Union in accordance with its own constitutional requirements. The withdrawal process involves a two-year negotiation period, extendable by unanimous agreement of the European Council.
  *Heimild: Treaty on European Union, Article 50*
  Fyrirvarar: While withdrawal is legally possible, the Brexit process (2016–2020) demonstrated significant political, legal, and economic complexity. The UK's withdrawal agreement negotiations took over 4 years from referendum to final departure.

- **SOV-LEGAL-024** (líkindi: 0.56)
  The Nordic countries vary considerably in their approach to post-employment restrictions for government officials, but several have stricter frameworks than Iceland. Denmark: The karantænelov (Act on Certain Restrictions on Ministers' and State Secretaries' Transition to the Private Sector, adopted 2019) imposes a mandatory 6-month general cooling-off period for ministers and state secretaries after leaving office, plus additional restrictions of up to 12 months if their new position relates to their former portfolio. An independent advisory board (Karantæneudvalget) assesses each case. Finland: The valtion virkamieslaki (State Civil Servants' Act, 750/1994, amended 2017) allows ministries to include up to 12-month cooling-off clauses in senior officials' employment contracts. The practice is not universal but has become increasingly common for permanent secretaries (kansliapäälliköt) and directors-general. A 2020 government resolution recommends such clauses for all senior positions. Sweden: Sweden does not have a general cooling-off law for ministers, but the Nämnden för prövning av statsråds och statssekreterares övergångsrestriktioner (Board for Review of Transition Restrictions for Ministers and State Secretaries), established in 2018, can impose transition restrictions of up to 12 months when ministers or state secretaries move to the private sector. Norway (non-EU but relevant as an EEA comparator): The karanteneloven (Quarantine Act, 2015) is the strictest in the Nordic region, imposing up to 6 months of quarantine (karantene) plus up to 12 months of restricted activity (saksforbud) for ministers, state secretaries, and political advisors. An independent committee (Karantenenemnda) assesses each transition. By comparison, Iceland has no mandatory cooling-off period, no independent review body, and only non-binding guidance in the ministers' ethics code.
  *Heimild: Danish Karantænelov (2019); Finnish Valtion virkamieslaki 750/1994; Swedish SOU 2017:3; Norwegian Karanteneloven (2015); OECD Post-Public Employment report (2022)*
  Fyrirvarar: The effectiveness of Nordic cooling-off regimes varies. Norway's system is considered the most robust due to mandatory application and an active independent committee. Denmark's 2019 law is relatively new and has had limited case law. Finland's approach is contractual rather than statutory, meaning coverage depends on individual employment agreements. Sweden's board can only recommend restrictions, though recommendations have been followed in practice. Direct comparison with Iceland is complicated by Iceland's much smaller government apparatus — the potential revolving-door pool is smaller, but so are the degrees of separation between government and the private sector, which arguably makes the risk higher per capita.

- **SOV-LEGAL-023** (líkindi: 0.558)
  The EU Treaty framework imposes specific post-employment obligations on senior EU officials. Article 245 TFEU (Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union) states that members of the European Commission shall, both during and after their term of office, 'respect the obligations arising therefrom and in particular their duty to behave with integrity and discretion as regards the acceptance, after they have ceased to hold office, of certain appointments or benefits.' The European Commission's Code of Conduct for Commissioners (revised 2018) operationalises this: departing Commissioners face a 2-year cooling-off period (3 years for the Commission President) during which they must notify the Commission of any intended new professional activity and obtain approval. If the activity relates to their former portfolio, it is presumptively prohibited during the cooling-off period. The European Ombudsman has investigated several cases of alleged revolving-door violations, most notably the move of former Commission President José Manuel Barroso to Goldman Sachs in 2016, which led to the strengthening of the code in 2018. Despite these rules, enforcement remains contested — the Commission's own ad hoc ethics committee assesses notifications, but critics argue it lacks independence. The European Parliament has repeatedly called for the establishment of an independent EU ethics body.
  *Heimild: Article 245 TFEU; European Commission Code of Conduct for Commissioners (C(2018) 700); European Ombudsman Decision OI/1/2021/KR*
  Fyrirvarar: EU revolving-door rules apply to Commissioners and senior EU staff, not to member state officials in Council formations or national representatives. If Iceland joined the EU, its Commissioner would be subject to these rules, but Icelandic ministers attending Council meetings would not. The rules have been criticised both as too weak (by transparency NGOs like Transparency International EU) and as too intrusive (by those who argue they deter qualified candidates from serving). The 2018 strengthening was partly a response to the Barroso controversy but has not been tested in a comparable high-profile case since.

- **SOV-DATA-007** (líkindi: 0.539)
  Many Western democracies have stricter post-employment (revolving door) restrictions than Iceland for senior government officials. Examples include: France requires a 3-year cooling-off period for senior officials before joining companies they supervised, enforced by the Haute Autorité pour la Transparence de la Vie Publique (HATVP); Canada imposes a 2-year post-employment restriction for ministers and 1 year for senior officials, enforced by the Office of the Conflict of Interest and Ethics Commissioner; the European Commission imposes a 2-year cooling-off period for departing Commissioners (extended to 3 years for the Commission President), during which they must notify and receive approval for any new professional activities; the United Kingdom has an Advisory Committee on Business Appointments (ACOBA) that reviews post-employment moves by ministers and senior officials, typically recommending 6–24 month waiting periods (though compliance is voluntary). By contrast, Iceland has no mandatory cooling-off period for ministers or senior officials, a gap highlighted in GRECO evaluations.
  *Heimild: OECD — Managing Conflict of Interest in the Public Sector; GRECO evaluation reports; European Commission Staff Regulations*
  Fyrirvarar: The effectiveness of cooling-off periods varies widely. France's HATVP is considered among the most robust enforcement mechanisms, while the UK's ACOBA has been criticised as toothless (its recommendations are advisory). The EU Commission's own rules have been criticised as insufficient after several former Commissioners moved to private sector roles in related areas. Stricter rules do not guarantee compliance — enforcement capacity is often as important as the rules themselves.

- **EEA-LEGAL-017** (líkindi: 0.531)
  The European Commission's official enlargement documentation explicitly describes accession negotiations as being about 'the conditions and timing for the candidate's adoption, implementation and enforcement of all current EU rules (the acquis).' The Commission's factsheet 'EU Accession Process' (updated 2022) states the process involves: (1) screening of each chapter against national legislation; (2) setting opening and closing benchmarks; (3) negotiating transition arrangements where needed; and (4) monitoring implementation. At no stage does the candidate negotiate changes to the acquis itself. The EU Council's negotiating frameworks for current candidates (Montenegro, Serbia, Albania, North Macedonia, Ukraine, Moldova) all contain the standard clause that 'the candidate accepts the acquis as it stands at the time of accession.'
  *Heimild: European Commission — 'EU Accession Process: Steps towards joining' factsheet*
  Fyrirvarar: While technically accurate, this framing understates the genuine flexibility that exists within transition arrangements. Transition periods can be economically and politically significant (e.g., delayed free movement of workers after the 2004 enlargement lasted up to 7 years). The distinction between 'negotiating the rules' and 'negotiating when to apply the rules' is real but can be overdrawn.

- **EEA-LEGAL-007** (líkindi: 0.521)
  The average time between EU adoption of a legal act and its incorporation into the EEA Agreement via Joint Committee Decision has been approximately 12–18 months, though backlogs have at times exceeded 500 outstanding acts. As of 2024, the EFTA Surveillance Authority reported an incorporation backlog of around 400–500 acts awaiting Joint Committee decisions.
  *Heimild: EFTA Surveillance Authority — Annual Report 2024*
  Fyrirvarar: The backlog fluctuates and has been a persistent concern. Some delays are due to constitutional requirements in EFTA states (particularly Norway and Iceland requiring parliamentary approval for certain acts). Critics argue the lag creates legal uncertainty; defenders note it allows adaptation time.

- **SOV-LEGAL-020** (líkindi: 0.519)
  Iceland has an established legal framework for managing conflicts of interest in government. Key instruments include: (1) Lög um varnir gegn hagsmunaárekstrum í Stjórnarráðinu (Act on Prevention of Conflicts of Interest in Government Offices), which requires disclosure of financial interests and imposes restrictions on officials' participation in decisions where they have personal interests; (2) Siðareglur ráðherra (Code of Ethics for Ministers), which establishes standards of conduct for cabinet members; (3) General administrative law principles on disqualification (vanhæfi) under the Administrative Procedures Act (stjórnsýslulög nr. 37/1993). However, Iceland does not have a post-employment cooling-off period requirement for ministers or senior officials — there is no mandatory waiting period before former officials can take positions in the private sector or with international organisations. This gap has been highlighted in international assessments, including GRECO (Group of States against Corruption) evaluation reports.
  *Heimild: Lög um varnir gegn hagsmunaárekstrum í Stjórnarráðinu; GRECO Fourth Round Evaluation Report on Iceland*
  Fyrirvarar: While the legal framework exists, enforcement and coverage have been criticised as insufficient by both domestic commentators and international assessment bodies. The absence of a mandatory cooling-off period is the most commonly cited gap. The Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn's proposed 18-month ban bill (SOV-LEGAL-007) is a response to this gap.

## Fullyrðing 3 (claim_id: 63)

**Íslenskur texti:** Þjóðaratkvæðagreiðsla er betra og skilvirkara samráð en samráð við utanríkismálanefnd, að mati utanríkisráðherra.
**Flokkur:** sovereignty

**Heimildir úr staðreyndagrunni:**

- **SOV-LEGAL-019** (líkindi: 0.538)
  Article 24 of the Icelandic Parliamentary Rules of Procedure (lög um þingsköp Alþingis, nr. 55/1991, with subsequent amendments) establishes that the government shall always consult the Althingi Foreign Affairs Committee (utanríkismálanefnd) on important foreign affairs matters before making decisions. The article states: 'Ríkisstjórnin skal ávallt bera meiri háttar utanríkismál fyrir fram undir nefndina' (The government shall always bring important foreign affairs matters before the committee in advance). The committee consists of members from all parliamentary parties and serves as the primary parliamentary oversight mechanism for foreign policy. The provision is designed to prevent unilateral executive decisions on foreign affairs without parliamentary consultation. Whether a specific matter qualifies as 'meiri háttar utanríkismál' (important foreign affairs matter) is subject to interpretation, and disagreements between government and opposition on this classification have been a recurring feature of Icelandic parliamentary politics.
  *Heimild: Lög um þingsköp Alþingis nr. 55/1991; Alþingi official website*
  Fyrirvarar: The article establishes a consultation requirement, not a veto power for the committee. The government retains decision-making authority on foreign affairs — the committee must be consulted but its approval is not legally required. Whether submitting a parliamentary resolution (þingsályktunartillaga) to hold a referendum constitutes an 'important foreign affairs matter' requiring prior committee consultation is a matter of legal interpretation on which reasonable lawyers disagree.

- **POLL-DATA-010** (líkindi: 0.482)
  The wording of EU-related poll questions significantly affects measured support. When asked 'Should Iceland apply for EU membership?', support is typically 3–5 percentage points higher than when asked 'Should Iceland join the EU?', and 5–8 points higher than when asked 'Are you in favour of EU membership?'. The application question implies a process with future decision points, while the membership question implies finality. The binding referendum question — 'Viljið þér að Ísland gerist aðili að Evrópusambandinu?' ('Do you want Iceland to become a member of the European Union?') — uses the direct membership framing, which historically produces lower support levels. Pollsters using different wordings have produced divergent results on the same day by up to 10 percentage points.
  *Heimild: Félagsvísindastofnun — Methodology notes on EU polling; comparison of Gallup, MMR, and Maskína polls*
  Fyrirvarar: Question wording effects are well-documented in referendum polling internationally. The official referendum question was set by legislation and cannot be changed. Pre-referendum polls using different wording should be interpreted with caution when predicting the actual vote.

- **POLL-DATA-020** (líkindi: 0.481)
  The Gallup Þjóðarpúls survey (19 February – 4 March 2026, n≈810) found that a majority of Icelandic respondents supported holding a national referendum on resuming EU accession negotiations. According to the published results, roughly 60% of respondents supported the referendum, approximately 30% opposed it, and approximately 10% were undecided or did not respond. The survey found sharp party-based divisions: voters of Viðreisn (Reform Party) and Samfylkingin (Social Democrats) were the most strongly supportive, while voters of Miðflokkurinn (Centre Party) and Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn (Independence Party) were the most strongly opposed. The survey measured support for holding a referendum on resuming negotiations — not support for EU membership itself, which typically polls lower. This distinction is important: a voter may support giving people the chance to vote on the question while personally opposing EU membership.
  *Heimild: Gallup hf. — Þjóðarpúls; Vísir*
  Fyrirvarar: Pre-referendum polling can diverge significantly from actual voting outcomes. Iceland's small population means that campaign dynamics, events, and high-profile endorsements can shift opinion rapidly. The distinction between 'support for a referendum' and 'support for EU membership' is crucial — historically, support for holding a vote runs higher than support for the substantive position. Poll results are also sensitive to question wording and ordering effects.

- **POLL-DATA-008** (líkindi: 0.48)
  The Gallup Þjóðarpúls March 2026 survey found notable demographic patterns in EU referendum attitudes. Women were more supportive of continuing negotiations than men. Residents of the capital area (Höfuðborgarsvæðið) were more supportive than those in rural areas. University-educated respondents were more supportive than those with less formal education. By party affiliation, voters of Viðreisn and Samfylkingin were most supportive of resuming negotiations; Miðflokkurinn and Sjálfstæðisflokkurinn voters were most opposed. A notable finding was that nearly all respondents who oppose holding the referendum would vote to stop negotiations if one were held, but a significant minority who support holding the referendum would also vote against continuing negotiations — indicating that support for democratic process does not equate to support for the policy outcome.
  *Heimild: Gallup Iceland / Þjóðarpúls*
  Fyrirvarar: Specific percentage breakdowns by demographic group are not included here as they were not available in the public summary. The demographic patterns (gender, education, urban/rural) are consistent with EU attitude surveys across Nordic countries and may reflect underlying structural factors rather than campaign effects. Party-affiliation breakdowns should be interpreted cautiously as some parties have small voter bases in the sample.

- **POLL-DATA-011** (líkindi: 0.477)
  EU support varies dramatically by party affiliation. Among Viðreisn voters, ~90% support EU membership; among Samfylkingin voters, ~75%. Píratar voters show ~55% support, and Left-Green voters ~45%. On the opposing side, Sjálfstæðisflokkur voters show ~25% support, Framsóknarflokkur voters ~20%, Miðflokkur voters ~10%, and Flokkur fólksins voters ~10%. - **POLITICAL-DATA-012** (líkindi: 0.477)
  Þórdís Kolbrún Reykfjörð Gylfadóttir, leader of Viðreisn (Reform Party), serves as utanríkisráðherra (Foreign Minister) in the coalition government formed in late 2024. She has been Viðreisn's leader since 2022 and was first elected to Althingi in 2016.
